Nearly 40% of the nation’s entrepreneurs are non-Caucasian. Minorities own 15.1% of all U.S. businesses, or more than 3 million firms, and 99% of these firms are small businesses. More than one-third of minority-owned firms are owned by Hispanics.

Source: Forbes, Entrepreneur

Immigrants are a major driving force in the entrepreneurial scene. 80 percent of immigrant high-tech entrepreneurs operate in the twenty-five largest U.S. metropolitan areas, compared with only 57 percent of their American-born counterparts.

Source: Forbes, Kauffman Foundation Thoughtbook 2015

The gender gap is slowly closing, though male-owned still outnumber womenowned business from 2 to 1. However, more women are making the leap into entrepreneurship, including Millennial moms. In fact, 69% of new businesses start at home.

Source: Forbes, Babycenter

Nearly 30% of entrepreneurs are between the ages of 20-34. Millennials are underemployed but daring risk-takers, highly-educated yet straddled with student loan debt, were raised with technology and new media. In addition, they are environmentally and health-conscious, which have emerged as more than lifestyle trends, but reforming cultural norms.

Source: Forbes, Babycenter

One in four Americans ages 44-70 are interested in starting their own businesses in the next five to 10 years. Longer and healthier life spans and the increasing need to actualize an unfulfilled desire to become a first-time entrepreneur or in some cases, serial entrepreneurism is a common factor. Forbes contributor and entrepreneurship author, Marty Zwilling, calls entrepreneurship the new “mid-life crisis.”

Source: Forbes, Encore
